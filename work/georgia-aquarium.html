Dining at Georgia Aquarium

UX Research, UX Design


Introduction

The Georgia Aquarium, home to over 100,000 animals, offers engaging and exciting guest experiences that inspire the awareness and preservation of aquatic animals worldwide. In 2018, around 2.5 million guests visited the aquarium, and summer visitor attendance reached record breaking numbers. The client has projected that the numbers are going to continue to increase, which will add stress to the exhibit and gallery spaces as well as the dining areas.

Therefore, the design space of this project is exploring the possible solutions to alleviate overcrowding issues faced in Café Aquaria. This topic space can be broken down to the following central ideas:

  • The nature of Cafe Aquaria’s ordering and dining experience as it currently exists
  • The needs and pain points of the aquarium visitors
  • The aspects of Cafe Aquaria that are functioning well
    • Considering these central ideas, we summarized our problem statement as:
    “How might we design a clear, efficient and enjoyable dining experience at Cafe Aquaria?”

    About our users

    observation

    Why observation?

  • By viewing individuals or groups of customers, we could collect information in a natural way without disturbing them. Thus, we thought we could get more accurate information.
  • It is also efficient, as you can follow a group of people finishing their ordering no more than ten minutes.
  • Another advantage is that it is thorough. As we split into groups to observe different areas concerning different dining stages, we could get a comprehensive picture of user journey.
    • Afterwards,we made an affinity diagram and found patterns of customer needs and the challenges they faced.
    We got five green notes that are customer motivation, multitasking parents, information needs, flow of traffic, and seating needs. Among them, the information needs, traffic flow, and seating needs are the most common customer demands and are pain points caused by the current service design of the cafe.

    see detailed affinity diagram on Miro
    staff interviews

    Why staff interviews
    Cafe staff will most likely interact with the system we design, benefit from its use, and also provide feedback on design iterations. Establishing a relationship with the staff through interviews not only gives us insight into the operations of the cafe but is also useful in the ideation and testing phases of the project where we need a pool of users to draw from. We chose to use a semi-structured interview format because it is typically used in the early stages of research.

    Results
    We interviewed 1 manager, 3 cashiers, 1 floor attendent supervisor and 1 floor attendent, and developed better understanding of the schedule, training, team dynamic, current technology, emotions, top concerns of the staff. They also made comments about the top concerns of the customers: first the pricing, second the seating.
    The most noticeable point for our design is the lack of trust: customers don't trust the cashier, so they sometimes ask the cashier to reweigh their food to check the price; sometimes they'll toss the food or putting it back when finding the price too high, creating more food waste.

    touring interviews

    Why touring interviews?
    After analyzing our observation data, we found that we lacked specific details on the ordering process and motivation behind user behaviors. Based on our professor'suggestions, we went back to the aquarium the next weekend to conduct touring interviews.
    Specifically, touring interview is observing a customer from when they pick up a tray until they pay for food and find seating, and taking notes on the process yields actionable data that can be followed up on with semi-structured interviews. This method is essentially a combination of both observation and follow-up interviewing in order to truly capture a customers ordering process, including their pain points and frustrations.

    Results
    Interview findings were very similar to our observations; instead of reavling more insights and patterns, these interviews confirmed some of our hypotheses during interviews. While most of the interviews gave us valuable data, we found that one customer in particular was only concerned about receiving the incentive and was unresponsive during the interview.

    competitor analysis

    Why competitor analysis?
    Naturally, each cafe will have its own unique characteristics given the aquarium location, size, and budget. By conducting a competitive analysis, we can quickly see the strengths and weaknesses across multiple cafes and how they compare with each other. We had little knowledge of how other aquarium cafes were implemented beyond Cafe Aquaria, so we were curious if there were any best practices for aquarium cafes in general. The characteristics of different cafes can help us be aware of certain design decisions and trade-offs a cafe may have chosen to better accommodate their customers.

    Results
    We then went on the websites of each of these aquariums to look up the cafes to see their menus, environment, and what they had to offer. Furthermore, we also looked up the TripAdvisor and Google Map photos, ratings, reviews of the cafes within each aquarium. For the competitor analysis, we set up a cafe features matrix listing items that we thought were important in having an effect on the cafe experience, as well as features that some cafes had in common and filled in the table accordingly.     Sketched concepts

    pre-order kiosk

    Self-ordering kiosks allow customers to order food themselves, and serve as an additional sales channel to the existing check out system. They can increase customer throughput during peak traffic hours, and increase sales by offering visitors another way to pay for their purchases at the cafe. The kiosks give information to customers about what is available without having to enter the cafe, so they can make better informed decisions, faster.

    interactive digital display

    The research we conducted in the previous phase revealed that customers do not have the information they need to make well informed decisions.

    Currently there is an interactive digital display that is placed outside the cafe entrance which provides information about what items are available on the menu. However, it is rarely used because the rotating ads force customers wait to see the info, and the menu info is inconsistent with what is actually served at the cafe, leading to customer confusion and frustration.

    This redesign opportunity prompted the formation of an interactive digital display concept that tackles the problem of overcrowding by:

  • Making the pricing system more transparent so that customers spend less time at the check-out area
  • Communicating the food choices to customers before they enter the cafe reducing dwell times at each station
  • Displaying the location of important places like the condiments and seating areas to give customers a mental model of the cafe layout before they enter the space

    Feedback on sketched concepts

    Participants were recruited from a pool of Georgia Tech students and interviewed through a semi-structured interview format. A trial interview helped us resequence the feedback session, including the format of the slide deck presented and the question design.

    The sequence of the feedback session was as follows:
    1. Background on project: Outline the current customer experience at the cafe + State the goal of the project
    2. Present all solutions
    3. Conduct feedback session based on questionnaire
    4. Ask follow-up questions for other comments and suggestions 5. Closing remarks and thanks

    Overall, the ideas are ranked as follows: mobile app > kiosk > interactive digital display. Thus, we decided to focus on the pre-ordering mobile app design.
